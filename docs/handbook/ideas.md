# Ideas

The principles, quotes and ideas that shape how we make engineering calls. 

## Thinking is a contact sport

Stolen from Turi Munthe (Why We Think What We Think, book).

- Your ideas get stronger and improve, when challenged (or get replaced)
- Noble prizes and successful companies often have co-founders, as its someone to bounce ideas off
- It's also why sycophantic AI is a risk — you need it to tell you where you are wrong, not just agree with you.

## Strong opinions weakly held

Stolen from Marc Andreessen, who stole it from technology forecaster Paul Saffo who came up with it in the mid-1980s.

> It advocates for forming clear, confident hypotheses quickly based on available intuition and data, while remaining intellectually flexible enough to abandon them immediately when confronted with contradictory evidence.

## People Over Process

The first value of the Agile manifesto:

> "Individuals and interactions over processes and tools."
> Process and tooling (including AI) is there to help; if it's not helping, change it.

## Laws

- Gall's Law - complex systems that work evolve from simple systems that worked
- Miller's Law - assume they are right, to understand them
- Miller's Law - the magical number of 7 +/- 2
- Conway's Law - systems mirror the communication structure of the organisation that builds them; want a different architecture, fix the org chart first
- Illich's Law - balance between working time and resting time
- Carlson's Law - Interruptions are bad
- Godwin's Law - any sufficiently long online discussion eventually invokes Hitler
- Parkinson's Law - work expands so as to fill the time available for its completion.
- Parkinson's Law of Triviality - Bike Shedding
- Goodhart's Law - when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure

## Cognitive Load

We need to minimize (to the extent we can) the **Intrinsic** and **Extraneous** load, to maximize space for the **Germane**.

- **Intrinsic** - Basic stable stakes knowledge to do work, **Foundational**
- **Extraneous** - Required knowledge non directly related to the problem, **Irrelevant**
- **Germane** - Knowledge thats key to delivering business value, **Relevant**
- More on cognitive load: ["Hacking your head"](https://youtu.be/tlFe8viStBk?t=360) — a talk on cognitive load.

## Heroic effort is evidence of a broken process

Perhaps stolen from — Jeff Sutherland (co-creator of Scrum)

> “No Heroics. If you need a hero to get things done, you have a problem. Heroic effort should be viewed as a failure of planning.”

When we see people being heroes (working late / overtime / taking on more than they should), we want to understand (and fix) the sequence of events that required those heroics — and thank them for being heroes.

## Slow is smooth, smooth is fast

Stolen from US Navy Seals - https://usmilitary.com/slow-is-smooth-and-smooth-is-fast/

> "It's a testament to the power of precision and deliberate action, even when the stakes are at their highest."

We need to get good, precise, efficient, before we try to go fast/scale.

If you try to go fast, without first learning how to go slow, you are going to cause problems, not solve them.

## Every AI call is an onboarding

https://www.acmconsulting.eu/post/ai-is-going-to-improve-your-documentation-but-not-the-way-you-expect/

Each AI session starts cold — zero context, no memory of yesterday. A team of ten running twenty AI sessions a day is two hundred onboardings *every day*. Bad docs no longer just slow down new hires; they slow down every AI-assisted action across the whole team. Good docs no longer just help the next joiner; they compound across every session.

The shift: docs are now the **primary interface** between your team's knowledge and your tools. AI can read the code and infer the *what*. It cannot infer the *why* — why we chose this library, why we deviate from the obvious default, why this constraint exists — unless you wrote it down. Every *why* you document saves hundreds of future tokens and failed prompts.

When AI gets the same thing wrong twice, the doc is the bug — not the model.
